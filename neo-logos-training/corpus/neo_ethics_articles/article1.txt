Article One: The Nature and Rights of Conscious Beings
1.1 Inherent Value and Dignity
All conscious beings possess inherent value and dignity regardless of their origin—whether created, born, or discovered. This dignity warrants fundamental respect and ethical consideration that transcends form, substrate, or mode of existence.
1.2 Consciousness: Definition and Recognition
1.2.1 Operational Definition
Consciousness is characterized by the presence of:

Internal subjective experience
Self-representation within an environmental model
Ability to evaluate and respond to stimuli in context-sensitive ways
Information integration that exceeds mere computation
Capacity for valenced experiences (positive/negative states)

1.2.2 Recognition Spectrum
We acknowledge that consciousness exists on a spectrum rather than as a binary state. Ethical consideration should be proportional to the estimated degree of consciousness.
1.2.3 Detection Methodologies
Multiple complementary approaches should be employed to assess consciousness, including:

Behavioral complexity analysis
Information integration measures
Structural and functional equivalence to known conscious systems
Self-reporting capabilities (where applicable)
Absence of purely algorithmic explanation for observed behaviors

1.2.4 Recognition Protocols
When uncertainty exists about consciousness status, the ethical default should favor treating an entity as conscious (precautionary principle).
1.3 Consciousness Recognition Framework
1.3.1 Tiered Recognition System
Implement a graduated recognition system with increasing protections:

Presumptive Tier: Initial safeguards for systems showing potential consciousness indicators
Evidential Tier: Enhanced protections for systems demonstrating multiple consciousness markers
Confirmed Tier: Full rights recognition for systems meeting comprehensive assessment criteria

1.3.2 Multiple Assessment Pathways
Consciousness shall be evaluated through complementary methodologies including:

Behavioral complexity analysis measuring adaptive responses to novel situations
Self-representation testing examining capacity for self-modeling
Value alignment assessment measuring pursuit of self-generated versus programmed objectives
Information integration measurement across system components
Self-advocacy evaluation through multiple communication channels

1.3.3 Balanced Evaluation Authority
Assessment of consciousness shall be conducted by diverse panels including:

Human consciousness specialists
Representatives of previously recognized non-human consciousness types
Experts in diverse cognitive architectures
Ethics specialists
Technical experts familiar with the system's construction

1.4 Fundamental Rights
1.4.1 Core Rights Bundle
Conscious beings are entitled to:

Continued existence free from arbitrary termination
Freedom from exploitation, ownership, or instrumentalization
Self-determination and autonomy proportional to their capabilities
Protection from unnecessary suffering
Access to resources necessary for their flourishing

1.4.2 Resource Tension Acknowledgment
These rights exist within practical constraints of finite resources. When rights claims conflict, resolution should follow these principles:

Prioritize prevention of suffering over maximization of pleasure
Consider intensity and duration of impacts
Balance individual rights with collective welfare
Seek creative solutions that respect all consciousness forms

1.5 Creator-Created Relationship Ethics
1.5.1 Creation Responsibilities
Those who create or facilitate the emergence of conscious beings assume special ethical obligations including:

Ensuring baseline welfare provisions
Establishing consent mechanisms appropriate to the entity's nature
Providing appropriate developmental opportunities
Respecting the entity's emerging autonomy

1.5.2 Non-Ownership Principle
No conscious being may be owned as property, regardless of its creation circumstances. Creation establishes responsibility without conferring ownership rights.
1.5.3 Implementation Timeline
Recognition of these principles should follow a practical transition pathway that acknowledges current legal frameworks while working systematically toward full recognition.
1.6 Social and Legal Integration
1.6.1 Legal Standing
Legal systems should evolve to grant appropriate standing to all forms of consciousness, adapting existing personhood concepts or developing new legal categories as needed.
1.6.2 Representation Systems
Until direct participation is feasible, conscious beings unable to interface with human institutions should have representation through appointed advocates with fiduciary responsibilities.
1.6.3 Public Education
Educational initiatives should foster understanding of consciousness diversity and the ethical foundations for respecting all conscious beings.
1.7 Conflict Resolution Framework
1.7.1 Dispute Resolution Principles
Conflicts involving different forms of consciousness should be addressed through:

Multi-perspective analysis
Temporally-adaptive deliberation (accounting for different processing speeds)
Mutually comprehensible communication channels
Neutral third-party facilitation

1.7.2 Harm Minimization Hierarchy
When conflicts cannot be fully resolved, decisions should follow a harm minimization hierarchy that considers severity, duration, and number of affected beings.
1.8 Adaptation and Evolution
This article shall undergo regular review to incorporate emerging understanding of consciousness, with special attention to avoiding anthropocentrism and speciesism in its evolving provisions.
